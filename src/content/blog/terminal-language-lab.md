You hit an error. You skim it. You paste it into a translator, or worse, straight into a search engine without reading a single word of it. Then you copy whatever fix shows up first.

You've done this a hundred times this month. And every single time, you skipped a free English lesson.

## Your terminal produces more English than any textbook

Stack traces, compiler warnings, linter output, git messages, Docker logs — this is English you encounter every day, in context, tied to something you actually care about solving. That's the exact condition language learners pay for: comprehensible input, tied to real stakes. You already have it. You're just not using it.

The problem is the habit of pattern-matching instead of reading. You see a red block of text, your brain says "error, bad," and you jump straight to the fix without processing the sentence. That's the same avoidance habit that shows up everywhere else in your technical English — skip the hard part, route around it.

## Why this costs you more than you think

Error messages are written by engineers, for engineers, in dense, information-packed sentences. Learning to actually read them — not decode them — trains exactly the skill you need for documentation, PRs, and code review comments. Skip this daily rep, and you're leaving the easiest practice available on the table.

## How to turn your terminal into practice

1. **Read the full error message before searching anything.** Out loud, if you can. Identify the subject — what is actually failing.
2. **Name the error type in your own words** before looking at the fix. "This is saying the module can't find a file at this path" — that sentence is the rep.
3. **Keep a running list of the phrases that repeat** — "expected X but received Y," "cannot find module," "unexpected token." These show up constantly, and once you own them, half your error messages get instantly easier to read.
