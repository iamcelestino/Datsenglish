You open a README. Fifteen minutes later you're still on the "Installation" section, translating every line, and you still don't know if this library even does what you need.

A senior dev would have closed that tab in 90 seconds — with an answer.

## Skimming isn't a shortcut. It's a separate skill.

Junior devs read documentation top to bottom, linearly, translating as they go. Senior devs read documentation like a map: they scan structure first, then drop into the two or three sections that actually matter for their decision. This isn't about reading faster in English — it's about reading differently, and it happens to be much easier in a second language because you're not fighting the temptation to read every word.

## The 90-second pass

Look at headings only, not body text. Most READMEs follow the same skeleton: what it is, why it exists, how to install it, a minimal usage example, and — often buried — known limitations. Your first pass should touch exactly those five things and nothing else. You're not trying to understand the library yet. You're trying to answer one question: is this worth ten more minutes of my life?

The usage example is the highest-value paragraph in almost any README. It tells you more about whether the tool fits your problem than the entire prose description above it, and it's usually the shortest, easiest English on the page.

## Why this is a career skill, not a study trick

Evaluating unfamiliar tools quickly is something you'll do weekly as a developer — new libraries, internal services, someone else's half-documented script. If your only mode is slow, linear, translated reading, every one of those moments costs you disproportionately more time than it costs a native reader.

## Practice this deliberately

1. **Pick a README you've never seen.** Give yourself 90 seconds, headings only.
2. **Write one sentence, in English, saying what the library does and whether you'd use it.**
3. **Only then** read the full document, and compare your first guess to what you actually find.
