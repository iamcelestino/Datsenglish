Ask most Portuguese-speaking developers why they don't speak up in an English meeting, and they'll say "my English isn't good enough." Watch them for a month, and you'll notice something else: they don't speak up in Portuguese meetings much either when the stakes are high.

The English is the visible excuse. The habit underneath it is older and has nothing to do with language.

## Language problems are easy to blame. Avoidance is uncomfortable to name.

"I need to study more vocabulary" is a comfortable story. It puts the problem outside you, in a stack of words you haven't memorized yet, and it comes with an obvious, safe next action: study more. "I avoid speaking up because I'm scared of being wrong in front of people" is a much less comfortable story — but it's usually the true one.

You can test this yourself. Notice the last time you stayed quiet in an English meeting. Was it really because you lacked the words, or because you had the words and didn't trust yourself to use them without a mistake?

## Why this reframe matters

If the problem is vocabulary, the fix is passive: more input, more study, more time. If the problem is avoidance, the fix is active: you have to do the uncomfortable thing on purpose, on a schedule, before you feel ready. Waiting to feel ready is the trap — confidence in a second language comes from evidence that you survived speaking, not from having studied enough to feel safe first.

## Breaking the actual habit

1. **Name one place you avoid English on purpose** — a meeting, a Slack thread, a comment on a PR — not the vaguest one, the most specific one.
2. **Do the smallest possible version of it this week.** One sentence in a stand-up. One comment left in English instead of translated in your head first.
3. **Track the outcome, not the performance.** Did the world end because your sentence wasn't perfect? It didn't. That evidence is worth more than another week of vocabulary lists.
