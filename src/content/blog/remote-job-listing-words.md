You find a remote job posting that looks perfect. Then you read: "we're looking for someone who takes ownership and thrives in a cross-functional, fast-paced environment." You understand every individual word. You have no idea what the sentence means.

This is a different problem than vocabulary. It's **corporate dialect**, and it's rarely taught anywhere.

## Job listings are written in code

Recruiters and hiring managers use a small set of recurring phrases that carry specific, unwritten meaning. "Fast-paced" often signals limited process and shifting priorities. "Wears many hats" often signals a small team where you'll cover ground outside your job title. "Cross-functional" means you'll work directly with product, design, or sales — not just other engineers. None of this is in a dictionary, because it isn't really about English. It's about how a specific industry writes.

Non-native readers usually lose here in one of two ways: either they translate literally and miss the implied meaning entirely, or they skip the phrase because it feels vague and unimportant — when it's often the sentence that matters most for deciding if the role fits them.

## Why this matters more once you're remote

In a remote, async, text-first job search, the listing and the follow-up emails are doing the work a conversation would normally do. There's no hallway chat to clarify what "ownership" means at this specific company. You have to extract it from the writing, which means you need to read job-post English as its own dialect, not as general English.

## How to get fluent in this dialect fast

1. **Collect 15–20 real job listings** in your target role and highlight every recurring phrase.
2. **Write your own plain-language translation** next to each one — what do you think it actually implies about the day-to-day?
3. **Cross-check with a real source**, like a Glassdoor review or a conversation with someone who works there, to correct your guesses. The gap between your guess and reality is exactly what you're training.
