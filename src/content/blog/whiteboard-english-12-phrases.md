You're standing at a whiteboard. The interviewer is waiting. Your solution is correct in your head — but the moment you open your mouth in English, it comes out in fragments.

This isn't an English problem. It's a **missing toolkit** problem.

## Whiteboard interviews reward a small, repeatable vocabulary

Nobody expects poetry at a whiteboard. Interviewers are listening for a handful of signals: that you understand the problem, that you're narrating your thinking, and that you can name trade-offs out loud. That's it. And those signals map to a surprisingly small set of phrases you can reuse in almost any interview, regardless of the actual problem.

The mistake most candidates make is trying to translate their Portuguese explanation sentence by sentence. That's slow, and it breaks the moment you hit a word you don't know. The fix is the opposite: memorize the **connective phrases**, not the content. The content changes every interview. The scaffolding doesn't.

## The 12 phrases that carry 90% of a whiteboard session

Things like: "Let me restate the problem to make sure I understand it," "My first instinct is...", "The trade-off here is...", "Let me walk through an example," "This works, but it's not optimal because...", "Let me trace through this with a small input." These aren't clever — they're boring on purpose. Boring means automatic. Automatic means you can focus your mental energy on the actual algorithm instead of the sentence structure.

## Why this matters more than vocabulary size

A candidate with 500 English words who owns these 12 phrases will sound more confident than a candidate with 5,000 words who's building every sentence from scratch under pressure. Fluency under stress isn't about knowing more — it's about having fewer decisions to make in the moment.

## How to actually install this

1. **Write out the 12 phrases** in your own words, not a generic list. Say them out loud until they don't feel foreign.
2. **Practice with a real problem**, out loud, timed. The pressure is part of what you're training for.
3. **Record yourself once.** You'll hear exactly where you default back to translating — and that's the spot to drill.
